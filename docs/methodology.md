# Methodology

## Overview

This section describes the research approach used by the Zoning for Zero Foundation.

## Research Approach

In paleoclimatology, scientists use proxy data—indirect indicators like ice cores or tree rings—to reconstruct past climates that cannot be observed directly in the present day. These proxies work because they encode stable physical relationships between observable features and climatic conditions.

The same logic applies to urban climate impacts which occur not on a preset day but in the future.

Built environment structures create persistent causal relationships: density patterns shape lifestyle and mobility choices, building volumes determine material emissions, green infrastructure affects carbon sequestration. These relationships are not random—they follow stable, quantifiable patterns that can be measured, calibrated, and used for prediction.

Climate proxies transform spatial data into climate predictions by encoding these stable relationships. When we know a neighborhood's morphology, building density, and green coverage, we can predict its carbon trajectory—not because we've measured its emissions, but because urban form reliably determines emission patterns.

This is the key insight: **proxies make prediction possible before emissions occur.** A development plan exists only on paper, but as we know its spatial characteristics, climate proxies reveal its future carbon footprint. This shifts urban planning from reactive measurement to proactive design.

### Key Components

1. **Data Collection** - Systematic gathering of planning relevant data
2. **Analysis** - Application of statistical and analytical methods with references
3. **Validation** - Peer review and verification processes
4. **Documentation** - Comprehensive recording of findings
